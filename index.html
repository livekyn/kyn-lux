How targets are calculated

Vitamin D — 8,800 IU target

Anchored to meta-analysis of vitamin D sufficiency research. Adjusted for skin type (melanin blocks UVB), age (skin thins ~1%/yr after 40), deficiency status, and health goal.

Nitric Oxide — cardiovascular threshold

≥20 J/cm² UVA (~30 min midday) is the research-backed minimum for measurable cardiovascular benefit. Scales with age — NOS enzyme efficiency declines significantly after 60, so older adults require proportionally more UVA stimulus.

Serotonin — mood sufficiency %

Brain serotonin rises directly with bright light duration (Lancet, 2002). Target is % of maximum achievable daily lift. Female baseline serotonin synthesis is ~52% lower than male, making women more sensitive to light deficiency — adjusted accordingly.

KYN Lux · Evidence Base

Vitamin D synthesis UVB

UVB converts 7-dehydrocholesterol in skin → previtamin D3 → cholecalciferol (D3). Peak production occurs at solar noon when elevation exceeds 45°. Melanin, SPF, cloud cover, and age all modulate output. Meta-analysis suggests 8,800 IU as the optimal daily production target from sun exposure for most adults. Skin type VI requires up to 6× longer exposure than Type I for equivalent IU output.

Nitric oxide release UVA

UVA photons photolyze nitrite stores in the skin, releasing nitric oxide systemically. NO vasodilates arteries, lowers blood pressure, and improves endothelial function — independent of vitamin D. Research shows ≥20 J/cm² UVA (~30 min midday sun) is required for measurable cardiovascular benefit. NO bioavailability declines with age as nitric oxide synthase (NOS) enzyme efficiency falls — making sun exposure especially important for adults 50+.

Serotonin production Retina

Bright light entering the eyes via the retinoraphe tract directly stimulates the raphe nucleus to upregulate serotonin synthesis. Lancet (2002): brain serotonin production is directly proportional to prevailing bright sunlight duration (r=0.294). Serotonin is also the upstream precursor to melatonin — making morning light the root driver of both mood and sleep quality.

The 3 circadian windows

Morning: Bright light within 60 min of waking amplifies cortisol-awakening response by 35–50%, suppresses residual melatonin, and entrains the SCN master clock for the entire day.

Midday: Peak UVA/UVB window. Acts as a second circadian anchor, reinforcing the SCN signal and buffering against evening artificial light disruption. Also drives testosterone and nitric oxide synthesis.

Sunset (DLMO): Low-angle orange/red wavelengths trigger the Dim Light Melatonin Onset — the body's natural sleep preparation signal. Skipping this window is a primary driver of delayed sleep phase disorder.

Age & biological adjustments

Vitamin D: Skin thickness declines ~1%/yr after 40, reducing UVB absorption. Kidney 1-alpha-hydroxylase conversion efficiency also falls. Targets increase 15–40% for ages 50+.

Nitric Oxide: NOS enzyme activity declines progressively from age 40. Adults 60+ may produce 40% less NO per unit UVA than young adults. Target increases proportionally.

Serotonin: Female serotonin synthesis rates are ~52% lower than males at baseline, making women more sensitive to light deficiency. Post-menopausal women show further reduced light sensitivity.

Movement + fat-soluble amplifiers

Exercise increases peripheral blood flow to dermal layers, enhancing UVB absorption efficiency (+12%). Healthy fats consumed within 2 hours improve intestinal vitamin D absorption (+20%) since D3 is fat-soluble and requires lipid carriers for uptake. Reflective surfaces (water, snow, sand) increase incident UVA/UVB by 10–25%.
